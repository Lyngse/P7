The plain-breasted piculet (Picumnus castelnau) is a species of bird in the woodpecker family. It is found in the Ucayali and Amazon floodplains in eastern Peru. Its natural habitats are subtropical or tropical moist lowland forests and heavily degraded former forest.

Description[edit]

A small bird with a short tail, it grows to a length of 9 to 10 cm (3.5 to 3.9 in). The face is greyish and the nape and upper neck finely barred or scaled with olive. The mantle and back are olive, brown or grey with slight pale barring and a buffy tinge and the wings are a darker shade of-brown. The upper side of the tail is brown with the central pair of feathers white. The underparts are a plain pale buff or white. The sexes differ slightly in that the male has a black crown speckled with red and orange while the female has a plain black crown. Both sexes have chestnut irises, pinkish-buff orbital rings, greyish legs and a greyish beak with the upper mandible somewhat darker than the lower one.[2] The song is a descending series of high-pitched notes "Tree'e'e'e'e'e'e".[3]

Distribution and habitat[edit]

The species is endemic to South America on the eastern side of the Andes where it occurs in the western part of the Amazon basin. It is present in southern Colombia, eastern Ecuador, Peru and western Brazil at altitudes of up to 1,000 m (3,300 ft). It is found in the more open parts of humid lowland forests, flooded forests, riverside corridors, swamps, thickets and groves, as well as logged areas with secondary growth and wooded pastures. It often occurs in areas of young Cecropia and Mimosa trees.[2]

Ecology[edit]

The plain-breasted piculet usually occurs by itself or in pairs but occasionally joins small flocks of birds of mixed species. It forages inconspicuously in the mid to low canopy and may be detected from the pecking sounds it makes while foraging; its diet is unknown.[4] It probably breeds between May and July.[4]

Status[edit]

P. castelnau has been described as fairly common over most of its range, and common on certain islands in the Amazon. Although there is a decline in the quantity and quality of its rainforest habitat, it is a fairly adaptable species and has moved into secondary growth forest, degraded forest and even wooded farmland and gardens. The total population is thought to be steady and the International Union for Conservation of Nature has assessed its conservation status as being of "least concern".[1][2]
